# Introduction

Walking home one day from the train station, I came across a rather peculiar satchel. Opening the case, I found a
manuscript inside. I use the word "manuscript" loosely. It was a written document alright. But some parts didn't
make sense. I continued reading late into the night and far into the next day.

I present here the full document as is, with some annotations by the original writer and me to help clarify
what it is I believe is being read or interpreted. Either way, it is all here in black and white.

The author's name is John Landry. I only know this because the last page of the manuscript states his name with
a signature on it. John's signature, it was spelled out in what appeared to be a red substance. Blood maybe? I'm
not sure until I have it analyzed. I didn't want to wait for that though, it doesn't matter if it's blood or not.
What matters is this record needs to get out to the world. People need to read it. People need to understand it
to the best of their ability. It needs to be released.

# Chapter One

1.1 Time is everything. Ancient times, modern times, the future. It all comes down to one thing. The seconds on the
watch keep on ticking no matter who you are. I was born in the year of our Lord one thousand eight hundred and
six, on the fourteenth of October, in a small town called Essex in Hampshire County of Florida.[^1]

1.2 My mother, having died in childbirth, was not around to raise me. So it was up to my father to begin my 
education. My father did the best that he could considering our poor circumstances. We worked all day and well into
the night to keep the farm afloat. Farming was in my dad's blood, you couldn't keep him away from his farm work. Even
on the days he wasn't feeling good you would find him out working the fields.

1.3 After I graduated from high school, I planned on going to college. But the farm was too much for my dad to 
run alone, and he was too proud to hire help. So I decided to stick around and help out as long as he would 
have me. I know deep down inside my dad felt bad about me not attending college. But what else could I do?
So I stuck on, until the inevitable happened.

1.4 Dad died on my twentieth birthday, he was only fifty-two. I'll never forget how that day went. I had 
woken up early, dad usually made waffles on my birthday, and I was looking forward to them. I remember walking
downstairs to the kitchen. Dad was nowhere to be found. I checked the entire house, there was no sign of him.
I went outside to the barn and found my dad's lifeless body there. Some wood had fallen loose from the rafters
crushing him and killing him instantly. At least I *think* it was instantly. Either way, he had been dead for
hours. He probably got up earlier to do his chores so he would have time for my birthday. There's not a day that
goes by where I don't blame myself for his death.

1.5 I held onto the farm and continued my dad's legacy. After his death, the farm reminded me so much of the good
times we had together. Working together. Laughing together. All the things that makes life worth living. I never
decided to have a family of my own, for one specific purpose.

1.6 When I turned twenty-two, I decided to take a small vacation. I boarded a ship and sailed to England. Well I
*thought* I was headed to England. I have some ancestors there and I thought I would look around some cemetery
and see what I could find out about them. As luck would have it, the ship I was on got struck by lightning and sank
to the bottom of the ocean. All hands were lost. *Including* mine. That's right, I died when I was twenty-two. It's
no lie, I died on that ship in 1728.[^2]

1.7 The next thing I remember, I woke up on my family farm without any knowledge of how I got there. I sure didn't
come back by ship, I would have remembered that. It was like I was simply plucked out of the ocean and placed back
in my bed without a scratch on me. I couldn't fully explain it at the time, but I had to find out what had happened
to me. So, I tested a few theories.

1.8 I started out small at first. Testing my limits as it were. I cut myself, I still bled. That hurt, don't try
that as a go to if you ever find out you can't seem to die. I tried drowning myself in the bathtub. First I tried
to hold my breath. When I didn't pass out, I exhaled all the air from my lungs and inhaled water as quick as I
could. I died within seconds, and just like that? I woke up in my bed.

1.9 It would appear that I cannot die. Or, rather, if I *can* die I manage to survive the event and wake up with
memories of it all but without any harm to my body. The knife cut on my hand I made earlier that day? Fully
healed. I can't explain it. I only know that it happened, twice now.

# Chapter Two

2.1 I had some decisions to make. If I couldn't die, I couldn't age. At least that's what I deduced. I wouldn't find
out until years later, that I was right about that. I couldn't stay on the farm forever. People would grow suspicious
about a man who didn't seem to get older. The last thing I wanted to be was being accused of witchcraft. I wasn't
a warlock. We didn't need a repeat of the Salem Witch Trials.

2.2 A plan had to be made. I would have to move around every few years. It was the only option. This of course meant
I couldn't get close to anyone either. How do you explain to someone you love that you'll never grow old with them?
It's not an easy discussion to have with someone. They would never understand why. So relationships were out of the
question. A shame really.

2.3 I stayed just outside of town for another ten years before I sold the farm. I figured staying longer might
raise suspicion. When you're supposed to look thirty-two, but you still look twenty-two? Sure *some* people can
pull off a thing like that, but it wasn't common.

2.4 Selling the farm wasn't an easy decision. I thought about renting it, leasing it, something like that. But
that wasn't in the cards for me. When you've lived as long as I have, you tend to let things go easier and forget
about them. Soon the farm was just a distant memory.

2.5 Soon it was the 1800s. I found myself moving to Wast Virgina. It was a quiet town called Dunner. A strange
name if you ask me. But it was still a town that I was living in. I made a few, a very few number of, friends.
They weren't real friendships of course, no one knew my secret. I couldn't afford telling *anyone* that side of
me. At least, not at that time I couldn't.

2.6 I was a witness to the Louisiana Purchase. Not many people can say that! To be honest, it was quite boring.
Land sales are always quite boring when you're there in person. I'm sure someone will make a big deal out of it
later on in life, but at the time it was just a boring purchase that was made. The U.S. got the land it wanted
and that's all that mattered. I do think it cost too much, but what do I know? I'm a simple farmer. &c.[^3]

2.7 The 1800s were an ... interesting time. During the 1820s, there was quite a religious excitement. It seemed
like there were churches popping up on every corner. Each teaching their own version of doctrine and what they
considered to be the truth. Many people were manifesting visions and the like of seeing the Lord as well, they
were asking for a forgiveness of their sins and the like.

2.8 In all my time there, I never did align myself with *any* religion of any kind. Don't get me wrong, I believe
in God. But I don't think people are allowed to speak for him. Not like the prophets of old in the Bible.

2.9 At this point in my life, I found myself fancying a certain young lady. Samantha Ross. With the permission of
her father, as was done in those days, we were able to see each other. I called on her often, and we enjoyed each
other's company.

2.10 After a while, I found myself falling in love. Now this was against my own personal code as there was no way
of explaining my ... condition to anyone. I never expected that to happen. It just did. After a lengthy courtship
of three years, and with permission from her father of course, we were married on the fourth of October 1830. I
had never been so happy in my entire life.

2.11 In 1832, there were some people who came through town teaching of religion and a "restored gospel." They
called themselves "Mormons." I suspect that the title was given them by others, and they decided to adopt it. I
am not sure though, that could simply be speculation. Their leader, a man by the name of Joseph Smith, Jun. even
came into town with them for a short time.

2.12 During this time, this Joseph Smith taught about his own experiences with angels and the like. How he was
given some kind of ancient writings which he called "The Book of Mormon." My wife was fascinated with the teachings
of these Mormons and their beliefs. She was baptized into their church almost immediately without a second thought.
She asked me if I wanted to be baptized, with my experiences with drowning and dying in water I decided against it.
She, of course, didn't understand why.

2.13 The hostilities that seemed to follow the Mormons around caught up with them soon enough. The mobs called
the members of the LDS faith a cult. Now I don't know if that's the case or not, but they did a lot of harm to
the missionaries and the members of our little town. Beatings, tar and feathering, such evil things. I can't 
understand why people would want to harm other good decent people. My wife included.

2.14 The mob attacked us in our home. My wife was taken hostage. They slit her throat from ear to ear. God knows
what else they did to her before killing her. They killed me as well, and I woke up the next morning in my bed.
The mob was gone by then, I was so angry with them. It was time to move on again. I couldn't stay there, not when
people *knew* I had been killed. I was forced to move.

2.15 I wanted revenge against the mobs. This Joseph Smith was causing too much harm for one man. Whatever these
mobs think they had on him, I'm sure they were wrong. He seemed harmless enough. I felt drawn to them, well him
at least. I needed to protect him somehow, no matter the consequences. So, I followed the missionaries back from
where they had come from and aligned myself as a ... friend to the so-called Saints. I don't regret my choice to
go with them. Not one bit.

2.16 It seemed the Mormons kept running into problems no matter where they went. They moved from county to county,
state to state, and still the mobs came in aggression. As the years passed, Smith himself was wrongfully arrested
and held against his will. As far as I know, he was arrested on two different occasions. Most recent arrests
included in 1838 in Missouri when he was held in Liberty Jail. Then he was arrested again in 1844 along with
his brother for treason, they were taken to Carthage Jail. Joseph was killed in Carthage along with his brother.

2.17 After Joseph's death, the saints were lost without their leader. At that time, I chose to leave the saints
and go on my own way to California. I needed a fresh start there. Following the Mormons around seemed to only
cause more trouble than it was worth. I didn't want that on my conscience anymore. I packed my things in early
1849 and moved out to San Francisco, California and didn't look back.

2.18 The gold rush was quite a sight. People wanted to mine or pan for gold and become rich. Everyone was flocking
to California to see what they could make of themselves. I, for one, did not care for riches, I kept to myself. 
There didn't seem to be a reason for me to get involved with gold, not at that time at least.[^4]

# Chapter Three

3.1 San Francisco was an amazing city once. If you look at it today, in 2026, it pales in comparison to what once
was. Long before cable cars, horse-drawn wagons and carriages ruled the city. The roads were made from cobblestone.
Not at first of course, but they were eventually installed in 1884. These were truely the wild west of times as it
were.

3.2 If there was one thing that living on the run has taught me, I needed work. Any work would do really as long as
it paid my boarding and food. Just enough to get by. I got a job as a bartender. It was mostly during the slow 
hours, the lead bartender wanted the busy time for himself. I was fine with that.

3.3 One night at the bar comes to mind everytime I think about working there. It was my second day on the job, I
had one customer come in that had an interesting story to tell. His name was Ethan, Ethan McClare of the California
McClare's. One of the richest families in the state, and they made sure it was known.

3.4 When Ethan walked in the saloon, he looked around. It was dead as usual for that time of day. Not too many
customers downstairs and definitely no customers upstairs with the ladies of the night. Ethan walked up to the bar
where I was wiping it down with a wet towel. I asked him what he would have, he ordered a shot of whiskey. It was
a normal request, nothing out of the ordinary there. But then, he looked at me and said he knew me from somewhere.

3.5 Personally, I had never seen the man before in my life. No matter how hard I tried to change the subject,
Ethan swore he knew me. He just couldn't place *when* he saw me. I finally gave up and allowed him to think
about it. The answer eventually came. He had been part of the mob that attacked the Mormons in West Virginia.
I asked him about what he was doing in California if being part of a mob was his idea of a good time. He simply
shrugged and told me he wasn't interested anymore in the Mormons. Being part of a mob wasn't his idea of living.
He came back to California to help out with the family business, whatever *that* was. He never did tell me *how*
his family made their money.[^5]

3.6 As we talked, I learned a little about him, but not enough to go off of. I guess it didn't matter much. The man
clearly knew what he had done to me and my family in West Virginia. But he showed no actual remorse over it, and he
sure as hell didn't offer an apology for his actions. The past was that, the past. I didn't really expect an apology
from this man. But I *did* want to know what he wanted, besides whiskey, from the saloon.

3.7 It must have been after that fifth shot of whiskey that Ethan finally loosened up a bit. He let his guard down
and told me he was no longer part of the "family business." Everything he had led up to this had been, what a lie
of sorts? I wasn't sure what Ethan's game was. So I asked some questions.

3.8 Turns out, Ethan had been shunned by his family two days prior. He had been living on the street among the sick
and otherwise disabled, mentally insane, what have you in order to survive from the goodness of others. Ethan had
learned a few things from some of the "old timers" as he called them, one had been mangled pretty bad in a mine
blast from dynamite.

3.9 Finally, Ethan asked if there were any poker games being played that day. I told him the poker games didn't
start until six o'clock at night. That's when the *real* action started. Finishing his drink, Ethan nodded. He
wished me a good day and left the saloon. I wa sure to see him later that night, when a man wanted to play poker
he usually found a way to make that happen.

3.10 Five hours later my shift was coming to an end. I closed out the till for the next shift to take over and made
an account of all the transactions for the shift as was standard policy. Mr. Crandall had a very specific way of
running his business and had to have things just so. I abide by his rules. I didn't want to be fired.

3.11 Sure enough, right at six o'clock sharp, Ethan came back to the saloon and sat down at the main card table
where the poker game would be played. Pulling out a gold pocket watch, he looked at it. Yes, he was right on time.
Ethan waited patiently for others to arrive, at the sight of him they questioned what he was doing there. He
simply responded that he wanted to get in on the game. One man, Tom Cobb, asked Etahn if he had any money to
gamble with. Ethan once again produced the pocket watch. Tom offered Ethan three dollars for it. Ethan accepted
and the game began.

3.12 An hour later, Ethan not only won back his pocket watch, but he cleaned out the rest of the men playing cards.
At first, Tom blamed Ethan of cheating. One problem with that, he couldn't *prove* Ethan had been cheating. So
he dropped the theory. Tom shook hands with Ethan, called it a good game and congratulated him on winning.
Standing up from the table, the other gents walked away leaving Ethan alone with his winnings.

3.13 I warned Ethan that he might want to invest in a bank account. Someplace safe to keep his money while he
was in town, however long that would be. He agreed to take care of it in the morning. I left the saloon shortly
after the game had ended. I was tired and wanted to get a good nights rest that night. Most nights I would have
stayed out until at least eleven. The town never slept, the saloon was open twenty-four hours a day. There was 
always someone on staff there.

3.14 As I was walking home, Ethan caught up with me for a second to chat. He asked if he could stay with me for
the night. It seemed the local hotel *and* boarding house wouldn't rent a room to Ethan. His family name was too
much for them to handle. Ethan was clearly upset about it all. I could understand that. I agreed to allow him to
sleep over at my place for the night. He could figure things out in the morning.

3.15 We walked to my small apartment. It wasn't anything special. I had a couch Ethan could sleep on. He was so
grateful just to have a place to sleep that night. Ethan was tired, he laid down on the couch and fell asleep
almost immediately. I, on the other hand, had a bout of severe insomnia. What if something were to happen to me
and I died while Ethan was in my home? How would I explain *that* to him? I didn't want to find out. I decided that
allowing him to stay in my home was a bad idea. But I was stuck with him for the night and stuck with whatever
consequences came from it. I was terrified.

3.16 We ended staying up all night and simply talking. Not once did he bring up the mobs he had been part of.
I'm questioning if he was ashamed of it all. I can't say for certain, but I know if I had been part of that
misery, I would have major regrets. Instead, we talked about his childhood and upbrining making him the man
he was at that time. It was enlightening.

3.17 When morning came we parted ways. He grabbed what little possessions he had and went on his way. I don't know
where he went, but I never saw him again. It was a quaint meeting, that was for sure. I went to see the local
pharmacist to see if there was anything they could give me to keep me awake. Unfortunately they had nothing. A
shame really.[^6]

3.18 At noon I went back to work. Walking into the saloon I saw something that I hadn't seen the entire time I
had been in the city. The saloon was full. I mean there was standing room only and even *that* was difficult to
do. The main bartender had been called in to help cover until I got there. Going behind the bar, we exchanged words.

3.19 I asked him what was going on. He told me there had been a mine collapse. That meant no one could go to work
until the debris was cleared or the mine was closed and a new mine was carved out. So, people had nowhere to go.
They decided to grab a drink or six. I was astonished. Luckily no one was hurt in the collapse. They managed
to get the miners out to safety quickly.[^7]

3.20 When 1860 rolled around, there was talk of an upcoming Civil War between the North and the South. I set out
to fight in it. I was killed on the battlefield many times, too many to count. It was one bloody massacre after
another one. Doctors would finish cutting the leg off of one wounded soldier, wipe the saw on their bloody apron, 
and start cutting into another man. Like I said it was a bloody ordeal.

3.21 As the war was coming to an end, Lincoln signed the Emancipation Proclamation in 1863. It declared that 
all enslaved people in Confederate states still in rebellion "are, and henceforward shall be free" It was the
first step in freeing the slaves in the south.

3.22 After the war was finished in 1865, I made my way back to San Francisco and retook my job I had before the
blasted war had began. I had been through a lot. I didn't want to discuss it with anyone. So I kept to myself
regarding the war. It was a complete mess. I tried to get back to normal life, but the nightmares wouldn't go
away.

3.23 I learned how to make myself "age". I bought some supplies that helped me change the appearance of my face
over the years. Even managed to turn my hair a little white, I said it was from stress. People didn't question me,
I was just another one of them. I managed to stay in San Francisco for thirty years tending the same bar until I
grew bored with it. In 1880, I decided to pack up my things and move on. It was time to move on. I had my fill of
San Francisco.

3.24 Before leaving San Francisco, I went to the ocean and gathered some sand in a small glass vial. A momento of
my time spent there.

# Chapter Four

4.1 I moved to Georgia. Once I got there, I immediately went to the ocean and gathered some sand there as well. Now
I had sand from both the Pacific and Atlantic Oceans. It sounds silly, I know, but it wasn't silly to me. It was
just something I wanted to do.

4.2 With my new location, I didn't have to wear the makeup to age myself. I could start fresh. I just had to
do the math on when I was born because I was *still* only twenty-two, even though I had passed one hundred
years quite a while ago.

4.3 Even with the Civil War over and the slaves freed, there were still people who were mean against the black
people. They felt they had no rights no matter what the President of the United States said. The whole ordeal
made me angry. Why couldn't people just follow the president's order? It made no sense to me. No matter where I 
went I made it a point to be friendly to *everyone* no matter their skin color, their religion, whatever they were,
they deserved respect. It's how I was brought up. This racism nonsense had to stop.[^8]

4.4 I decided I needed a job in Georgia and a place to live. The typical survival methods I had grown accustomed
to. During the Civil War I didn't have a problem with having a place to live. We lived in tents, and the food? Well
if you can call it food. It was edible. I'll give it that. We worked with what we had, and it wasn't much. The war
taught me how to rough it. In a way I'm glad I learned that lesson. It would serve me well over the many years.

4.5 I found a job at a local pharmacy. I stocked their shelves with whatever shipments came in that day. It was
an easy job, nothing too crazy. I was in Atlanta by this point in time, so the town was good size wise. There
never seemed to be a dull moment. My place of employment? Jacobs' Pharmacy. It just felt like the right plafe for
me to work. I was treated well and the pay was good too. What more could I ask for?

4.6 One night, after work, I was walking home. I lived in a boarding house. That is, several bedrooms in one
house each with a key to lock your room. Meals were prepared and served by the lady who owend the boarding house.
The meals weren't free, but they were easy to pay for. Room and board was three dollars a month. Meals included was
another fifty cents. Like I said, it was very respectible. The landlord, a Mrs. McConally, was nice. She was a
widow. Her husband had died three years earlier, and she had been alone ever since. June *claimed* that she wasn't
feeling lonely, but her eyes told a much different story. We became good friends.

4.7 One friendship wasn't enough for me though. I wanted friends that I could go out and enjoy a drink with. People
that I could bullshit with, about anything really. Whatever we decided to talk about, we would talk about. That's
what I was missing in my life. I know I said I couldn't get close to anyone. But that didn't matter anymore to
me. I needed friends.

4.8 The next morning I asked the employees of the pharmacy. Some said yes, others said they weren't interested.
That's okay, not everyone wants to spend time after work with those you work with. I didn't dare ask the pharmacist,
I was sure he had better things to do than spending time in his off hours with his subordinates.

4.9 The first night out, we stopped at the local watering hole and admired the scenery. That is, the ladies of
the night. Greg decided to see what that was all about, we didn't see him for the rest of the evening. He would have
to tell us the story the next morning.

4.10 As we got our drinks, we sat down at a table. A poker game was being played nearby. The customers at the table
were being quite loud about their hands. Each man boasting they had the winning hand. I kept glaring at them, but
they continued carrying on. Damn people can't allow other people to enjoy a drink with their friends.

4.11 Taking a sip of my whiskey I began to loosen up a little. I wasn't as mad as I had been. It was going to be
a good night after all. So we talked. We talked about work, we talked about everything else. How the political
climate was. Things had changed since Lincoln's assassination. That much was certain. It was almost 20 years ago
now, but people still felt the sting of it.

4.12 We talked late into the night. The saloon was closing for the night, and we had to leave. Probably for the
best. Drinking the rest of my beer, I stood from the table. Wishing my companions a good evening, I headed out
the door. That's when I saw *him*. Or rahter heard him.

4.13 I'll never forget his words to me. "I know your secret." His voice trailed off as I turned to face the
accuser. Walking up to him, I asked him to repeat himself. Which he did in the same ominous tone. "I know your
secret." He locked eyes with mine. It had been awhile since I had died, there wasn't anyone in town who knew
anything about me. Not that I knew of.

4.14 I asked the stranger what he wanted of me. He didn't say anything ... at first. He only stared at me, like
he was looking into my soul. Finally, he spoke. He asked what it felt like to die and then come to life again.
Damn, he really did know my secret. I asked him if we could talk someplace a little more private. We went to his
house on the edge of town.

4.15 Once I was sure it was safe, and there wasn't an angry mob waiting to attack me, I walked inside his house
and eased up a little. He offered me some coffee, I accepted. We sat in his parlor room enjoying our coffee. He
asked me when I was born, I told him\. He was amazed at that. The fact that I was over one hundred years old, he
was astonished. I wasn't one hundred percent sure this stranger wouldn't turn me over to the local authorities.
Heaven knows what *they* would do to me. But something told me I could trust him.

4.16 He told me his name was Mark. Now I don't know if that was his actual name or just an alias. I took it at
face value and accepted that as his name. Mark promised not to harm me, he only wanted to know what I had 
experienced over the years I had lived so far. So I told him. Hey, what else could I do? I just hoped he wasn't
lying to me.[^9]

4.17 As the night grew late, it was time for me to go home. Mark understood. He promised to keep my secret and
said he would be in touch. There was so much more he wanted to know. One thing he did ask me was if I could tell
the future. I told him the truth, the future was beyond me. I wouldn't know about the future until I lived it,
just like anyone else. He seemed to accept that answer. I was grateful he didn't want to harm me. I breathed
a sigh of relief.[^10]
